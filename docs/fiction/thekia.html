Story 2b — The Night She Did Not Fly

Field Value
Canon anchor Story 2 · EndgameRooms scatter speech · NARRATIVE_LOGIC #8
POV Third person limited — Thekia
Length target 2,500–3,000 words
When Banishment night (parallel to Story 2)
Cast Thekia; Kaefden IV's parents (in basin-reflection); Vashirr (distant); Oceandale fishers

Synopsis

Thekia is already in Oceandale when the sky empties. Through a scrying basin she watches Nacastrum's courtyard blaze — portals, the father's death, the mother's abduction. She cannot reach the tower in time; she was grounded by her own law. She understands immediately: Vashirr is not saving anyone; he is breaking anchor-pairs. She writes three names in the registry and closes the book. One washes up in six days.

Scene beats

  1. Basin-light — Nacastrum screams in reflection; the ward hums at her back.
  2. Recognition — the liaison family torn apart; she whispers the ground-oath clause she wrote.
  3. Choice — run to Aylova with the refugees, or hold the shore line. She holds.
  4. Ward flares — Agromanian torches slide off the magehouse arc.
  5. Registry — three names; two struck through; pendant number circled.
  6. Epilogue — six days later, a guard: "Another one from the sky, eh?" She pours tea.

Theme

Witness without rescue. The mentor's guilt is geography.

Tone

Tragic, claustrophobic. One room, one basin, one woman who wrote the law that kept her from the courtyard.

Connections

Story 2 (What the Barbarians Did Not Do) · Story 4b (The Ground House) · Story 4 (The Old Mage's Question).

Key dialogue

  • "They took ground-oathed kin first — the anchors that kept sky-blood honest. I wrote that clause. I cannot unwrite tonight."
  • "I did not fly because I chose the ground. Do not call that wisdom. Call it arithmetic."

Story 4b — The Ground House

Field Value
Canon anchor NARRATIVE_LOGIC #8–9 · MagehouseRoom · CHARACTERS.md Thekia
POV Third person limited — Thekia
Length target 4,500–5,500 words
When Twenty years before KoN (Vashirr crowned) through one week before the beach awakening
Cast Young Vashirr; council colleagues; ground-liaison couple (Kaefden IV's parents, unnamed); Oceandale fishers; Agromanian pickets (distant)

Synopsis

Thekia votes yes on the Council's bitter compromise: every sky-heir must keep roots on the ground — a civic oath, a shore house, a pendant that registers sky to earth. She assigns Oceandale to the liaison family because the fishing colony is honest, exposed, and impossible to romanticize from a tower.

When Vashirr takes the crown at thirty-three, she is the only council voice who calls his Many Hands pamphlet sincere — and therefore fatal. Within a season she resigns, descends from Nacastrum, and opens the Oceandale magehouse she helped charter. She binds a Levitate-anchored ward to the threshold: not to hide, but to wait. If Vashirr ever scatters the sky, the heir will wash ashore where the earth-oath was sworn.

She does not know his face when he comes. She knows the pendant registry.

Scene beats

  1. Council chamber, floating Nacastrum — debate on ground houses. Thekia: "Towers teach mages they are priests. Shores teach them they are neighbors." Vote passes. She signs Oceandale into the registry.

  2. Academy corridor — Vashirr (young teacher) argues with her after class: magic belongs in soldiers' hands, not council ledgers. She answers: "Hands that never chose the gift are not hands — they are handles." He marks her as obstacle.

  3. Coronation year — public exposure of the Council. Thekia walks out before banishment; takes only books, a staff-blank (later topped with blue gem), and the Oceandale deed.

  4. Descent — first night in the shore house. She Levitate-binds the ward. A fisher knocks, afraid of the humming threshold. She says: "Burn won't cross. Neither will I, until I'm needed."

  5. The quiet years — letters to Aylova ignored; visits from sky-mages who think she's gone soft; she maintains the house, watches Oceandale fish, hears Vashirr's propaganda on the wind.

  6. The week before — Agromanian banners on the horizon. Oceandale burns. Mages fall from the sky like blue rain. She stands inside the ward line and counts: one will stay when the others run east. She does not sleep.

  7. Coda — door slams on a stranger in blue robes. "Mage to mage; Kaefden to Kaefden." She has been waiting twenty years for a face she does not recognize.

Theme

Exile as strategy, not surrender. The council's last agent chooses the ground before the king chooses treason.

Tone

Patient, political, intimate. No combat — only wards, votes, and one woman reading the registry while a city burns outside her line.

Connections

Story 4 (The Old Mage's Question) · Story 2b (The Night She Did Not Fly) · Story 9 (Let Us Go, My King) · CHARACTERS.md family table.

Key dialogue (preserve)

  • "I did not leave Nacastrum because I was banished. I left because I saw the sermon coming."
  • "The Council caged our pride in shore-houses. I made sure one door stayed open."
  • "You recognize one of your own, don't you? Mage to mage; Kaefden to Kaefden."

Story 9b — Seven Years of Rising

Field Value
Canon anchor KoN ending · SoC library/throne/war camp · anthology Good #2–6
POV Third person, episodic (4 linked vignettes)
Length target 6,000–8,000 words total
When KoN ending → SoC opening (~7 years)
Cast Kaefden IV; returned mages; Sylvian envoys; Restoration hardliners; SoC protagonist (unnamed, future druid)

Arc summary

Thekia does not retire. She becomes Nacastrum's living memory — librarian, quartermaster, and the woman who tells Kaefden the truth when court softens it.

Era Role What she does
Year 0–1 Restoration architect Rebuilds the library shelf by shelf; reconstitutes a lean Council (no secret cabal — public spell-glass sessions). Writes the pamphlet margin: Binding without consent leaves echo — Vashirr knew.
Year 1–3 King's counterweight Kaefden wears the crown; Thekia refuses a throne seat. "I placed you there. I will not sit beside what I placed." Trains portal crews; tallies Ring costs from the Aylova rally speech.
Year 3–5 Coalition quartermaster As Agromanian pressure returns, she runs field logistics from Aylova war camp — potions, ward-chalk, class kit. Purple robes traded for a field tabard but the same seal on every crate.
Year 5–7 Witness rail Sends couriers (anthology Good stories) to Oceandale fronts; demands Restoration hardliners hear messy testimony before clean myths. Vouches for strangers because she remembers what it cost to be one.

Vignette beats

I — Homecoming (week after KoN)

She walks silver streets while mages weep through the Ring. Kaefden stands in the courtyard where his father died. Thekia does not comfort him with poetry — she hands him a ledger: stones, timber, portal hours. "Crowns do not end scatter. They only decide who pays first."

II — The library she hated as a girl

Young Thekia thought books were cages. Old Thekia shelves Vashirr's Many Hands pamphlet face-out, cracked spine, underlined by someone else's hand. She adds her margin note. A druid recruit from Cataracta will read it in seven years and not understand half — then understand all at once.

III — The letter to Silvarium

Elders will not trust a crowned amnesiac. Thekia writes: "He remembered on the Ring. He screamed. He rose anyway." Professional respect, not romance. The Blade quest begins as her diplomacy.

IV — War camp east of the cookfires

She presses a potion into a scout's hands: "Don't report to the front empty-handed." She has heard Kaefden's pledge. "Don't make me regret vouching for you in the throne room." Same woman who slammed a door on a lost mage now opens the throne room for a lost druid.

Beat Thekia's action

Theme

Restoration is administration of grief. The saga's constant changes uniform, not purpose.

Prose draft — Ash stops in a clean arc

Opening vignette from Story 4b; third person, ~600 words.

The ward hums at the threshold the way a held breath hums in the chest — steady, unnatural, patient.

Thekia has not crossed it in eleven days.

Outside Oceandale, the harbor burns low. Not the grand conflagration of the first night — that has sunk into ash and rumor — but small fires, stubborn, eating pier wood and net sheds while Agromanian pickets prowl the roads and sky-mages wash ashore with empty eyes. The guard at the broken gate has stopped asking her name. He only asks, each dawn, whether the fish are still running through the ward line. They are not. Even the water seems to respect the arc.

She stands at the study window with the registry open on her lap.

Three names, written the night Nacastrum screamed in her basin: the liaison, the oath-bearer, the heir. Two struck through in her hand — one by portal, one by blue fire at a castle threshold she saw in reflection, not flesh. The third name she does not know yet. She knows the pendant number.

Sky–earth bond, registered Oceandale shore, the clause reads in her own younger script. Heir to Restoration line, Academy matriculation pending.

She had voted for that clause. She had told the council that floating pride would kill them. Vashirr had called her a sentimentalist in public and a prophet in private, which is worse.

The door behind her is barred from the inside. The door at the street — the one that will slam on a stranger — is not. That is the ward's cruelty: it keeps fire out and invites need in.

Footsteps on the cobbles. Not pickets — too slow, too lost. She closes the registry without marking the page and lets her fingers find the Levitate knot above the lintel. The hum rises half a tone.

A figure slumps against the ward line as if the air were a wall. Blue robes. Pointed ears. Young, or young enough. The harbor smoke has stained the hem. He does not knock. He may not know where he is.

Thekia watches from the window until her knees ache.

She could open the door gently. She could call out. Twenty years ago she would have. But the council is dead, the sky is empty, and the boy on the cobbles — if he is the heir, if the pendant is on his neck under the salt — must choose Kaefden before she gives him Levitate. Mentorship as inheritance, not rescue.

She walks down the inner stairs. The books stacked to the ceiling tremble when she passes — old habit, old show of power. Let the ward do the shouting.

At the threshold she speaks to the hinge, not to the street.

"Enough."

The door bursts open. Pages fly. Wind that is not wind slams the heavy wood behind a stranger who does not yet know he has been expected.

Thekia steps from the stacks and meets his eyes.

"You recognize one of your own, don't you?" she says. It is not a question. "Mage to mage. Kaefden to Kaefden."

She does not add: I wrote your mother's oath. I watched your father die in a basin. I left the sky so you could learn the ground.

That can wait until he answers what faction the mages represent.

Outside, ash stops in a clean arc west of the magehouse, as if the air itself refuses the burn.
